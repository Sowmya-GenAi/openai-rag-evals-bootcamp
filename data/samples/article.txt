The Quiet Rise of Vector Databases

For most of the last two decades, the canonical way to put data behind an
application was a relational database. Postgres or MySQL stored your rows;
your application asked questions in SQL; the database returned answers in
milliseconds. This stack worked because the questions were narrow: "give
me the orders for customer 42", "count today's signups", "find rows where
status = 'active'". Predicates over structured columns. Exact matches.

The arrival of large language models has changed the shape of the
questions. Modern applications increasingly ask things like "find the
five passages in this 800-page manual that are most similar in meaning to
the user's query". That kind of question doesn't translate cleanly to
SQL. You're not filtering by a known column; you're searching by
*semantic similarity* over a high-dimensional vector representation of
text.

Enter the vector database. A vector database stores embeddings — typically
512- to 3072-dimensional float arrays produced by an embedding model — and
indexes them so that "nearest neighbour" lookups stay fast as the dataset
grows. Open source projects like Chroma, Qdrant and Weaviate emerged
between 2022 and 2024, and managed services like Pinecone matured in
parallel. Today, virtually every retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
system runs on top of one.

What's interesting is how *boring* most production vector databases turn
out to be. They are, at heart, a library that wraps an approximate
nearest-neighbour index (usually HNSW), plus a metadata store, plus a
small REST/gRPC layer. The hard part isn't the math; it's the operational
plumbing: ingest pipelines, embedding versioning, index rebuilds, and
crucially, evaluation. Without a gold dataset and a metric, "the search
got better" is a vibe, not a fact.

The next interesting question is whether vector search collapses back
into the same general-purpose database that holds the rest of your data.
Postgres has had `pgvector` since 2021. SQLite gained vector support via
extensions. The pendulum that swung toward specialized stores may swing
back. For most teams, the right answer is unsexy: use whatever your
existing operational database supports, measure recall and latency
honestly, and only reach for a dedicated vector store when the numbers
say you have to.

The lesson, if there is one, is the same lesson the database industry
keeps re-learning every decade: new workload, new abstractions, eventual
consolidation. Vector databases are not magic. They are a specialized
index over an old idea — distance-based search — finally good enough to
build products on. The interesting work has moved up the stack, into
chunking, retrieval strategy, and evaluation. That's where the next
generation of tooling will be built.
